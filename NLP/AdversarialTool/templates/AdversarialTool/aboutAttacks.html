About the Attacks

BERT-Attack [paper] [code]

BERT-Attack is a greedy contextualized word substitution model. This attack contains two steps. First, the model ranks the words of the input in order of importance in determining the input's original label. Second, the model uses BERT to formulate replacements, which is advantageous for helping preserve fluency, grammar, and semantic consistency because BERT can view words in context. However, this attack can sometimes produce outputs with antonyms or with words irrelevant to the input, causing a semantic loss.

PWWS [paper] [code]

PWWS stands for Probability Weighted Word Saliency. This attack works by generating synonyms for each word in the input. Then, the model uses a word saliency algorithm to determine the order of word replacement by calculating which individual changes cause the greatest probability change in classification. PWWS has a low word substitution rate, helping maintain semantic consistency, but it does not use context when making substitutions.

Genetic [paper] [code]

Genetic algorithms are inspired by the process of natural selection, iteratively evolving a population of candidate solutions towards better solutions. This attack ranks each word by context in the sentence and generates synonyms for each. Then, the algorithm will produce a set of different outputs. If one of them changes the classification, the attack is complete. If not, a new child sentence is synthesized from sampling two of the now parent sentences with a fitness function. This process repeats until the attack succeeds. While genetic algorithms perform well in maintaining semantic consistency, they can be computationally expensive with more complex sentences.

SememePSO [paper] [code]

In linguistics, a sememe is defined as the minimum semantic unit of human languages. PSO stands for particle swarm optimization, in which particles in n-dimensional space move towards optimal positions based on the positions all the other particles find, inspired by the movement of a flock of birds or a school of fish. For this attack, a position is a possible output sentence and each dimension is a word in the input sentence. The algorithm substitutes words based on their sememes, iteratively making substitutions until a swarm of particles finds a position that changes the classification. Using sememes instead of synonyms creates a wider possible range of adversarial examples, but PSO algorithms can suffer from falling into local optimums and having low convergence rates.

VIPER [paper] [code]

VIPER is a visual perturber that randomly replaces characters in the input with visually similar characters. For example, feeding the sentence "I think this sentence might look weird." through VIPER results in "I thiňk ẗhis sênteňce mîghẗ ᶅook ẘêird." The classification changes from "fear" to "surprise". Visually, humans can ignore the altered characters and read the sentence essentially unchanged. However, this attack is less practical in most writing contexts.
